Trip log

Every trip saves a snapshot of the conditions the app saw at that moment. That snapshot is the point — it's what lets the scores be checked against reality later.

Trips are stored in this browser only — nothing is shared automatically. To pool data with someone else, have them export their JSON and import it here. Duplicates are ignored, so re-importing the same file is safe.

Water temperature readings

The lake has no live sensor, so this is estimated from air temperature and sun. Log the canal and the open lake separately — a 6 ft sheltered canal and 26 ft of open water genuinely diverge, and each gets its own fitted curve. Five or more readings for a body lets the app correct that curve's sensitivity, not just slide it up or down.

Check the regulations before you keep anything. Walleye at Gull Lake are managed with Special Harvest Licence tags — without a tag they're catch-and-release. Pike limits and season dates change too. Alberta sportfishing regulations

Where all of this comes from

Measured — pulled live

Open-Meteo forecast API, free and without a key, queried separately for each spot's coordinates. Supplies air temperature, mean sea-level pressure, cloud cover, wind speed, direction and gusts, precipitation, weather codes, measured shortwave solar radiation, and sunrise/sunset. Sixteen days of history plus four days ahead, hourly. Open-Meteo blends national weather-service models (ECMWF, GFS, ICON among them); it is not itself a forecaster.

Measured — from a map

Both spots' coordinates, the canal's orientation (145°) and the direction its mouth opens (232°), the shelf's facing (305°), and the 9.05 km crossing between them were read off satellite imagery and checked by calculation. The fetch tables — how much open water lies behind the wind from each of sixteen directions — were measured against shoreline distances on a 19 km lake. Those are estimates good to roughly ±20%; because wave height scales with the square root of fetch, a 20% error moves a score by about 10%.

Measured — by you

Water temperature readings you and others enter by hand, kept separately for the canal and the open lake, each fitting its own correction curve. Everything else about water temperature is modelled, not observed — see below.

Five years of your own results

The most important input here, and the one that overrides everything else. About 98% of fish caught in the canal are pike, and about 95% of fish caught at the shelf are walleye. The app treats species presence as a property of the place rather than something weather decides, because that is what the record shows.

Strictly this is presence combined with how each spot gets fished — the shelf deep from a boat, the canal shallow and weedy. That is the right thing to model, since the question is what you will actually catch.

Published research

Alberta Conservation Association creel surveys of Gull Lake (2022) put walleye at roughly 1.25-1.70 fish per hour against pike at about 0.19-0.20. That figure is lake-wide and dominated by boat anglers over open water. An earlier version of this model used it to bias everything toward walleye, which was a mistake: it describes the lake, not a weedy 6 ft backwater. It is kept here for context, not as a weighting.

Species habitat preferences follow general fisheries literature: pike as structure-oriented ambush predators of shallow vegetated water that slow down above roughly 21 °C, spawning in flooded vegetation immediately after ice-out; walleye as low-light fish of deeper water and hard bottom that avoid dense weed.

Calculated here, no source

Water temperature is modelled from a four-day exponentially weighted mean of daily air temperature, adjusted for sunshine, then corrected against your thermometer readings. Turbidity is inferred from recent wind and rain with a decay half-life, never measured. Moon phase and transit times are computed in the page from the date. None of these are observations.

Assumptions — the weakest part

Every factor weight in the scoring model is a judgement call informed by general fishing knowledge, and none of it has been validated against results from this lake. Nobody has confirmed that a 72 here out-fishes a 45. That is what the trip log is for.

The model's strongest new claim, and the easiest to falsify: canal pike should peak from ice-out through spring, fall away through the midsummer heat as shallow water warms past what a cool-water fish is comfortable in, and come back in autumn. If the trip log shows a flat pike catch rate across the season, that seasonal shape is wrong and should be pulled out.

Two deliberate departures from fishing convention: barometric pressure is used only to identify where you sit in a weather front rather than treated as a cause, since a fish moving a few feet vertically experiences far more pressure change than any weather system delivers, and one controlled feeding study found no significant effect. And the moon carries a small weight, clearly marked, because the evidence for solunar timing is weak.

Infrastructure

Hosted on Netlify. The shared trip and temperature log lives in Netlify Blobs behind a small function; your browser also keeps its own copy so the page works offline. No account, no tracking, no analytics, and nothing leaves for anywhere else.

Scores are an informed guess, not a measurement. Wind and storm readings are a planning aid, not a boating safety call.
